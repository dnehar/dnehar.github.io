Research Focus

Immune Signatures of Early Life Immunity

Our lab leverages systems immunology to extract genomic signatures of immune responses to infection, vaccination, and various diseases, with a focus on translational pediatric immunology. Through close collaborations with clinicians, we identify mechanisms that can drive precision medicine, particularly in vulnerable populations.

Why pediatric immunology? Infants and young children are especially vulnerable to infectious diseases.

Despite this, the unique features of infant immune responses remain poorly understood. For example, while infants are more susceptible to respiratory infections such as influenza and RSV, they mount effective immune responses to other respiratory viruses like SARS-CoV-2, often developing milder symptoms. By defining early immune signatures in health and disease, our program aims to advance more effective pediatric treatments and preventive measures.

Research Focus · Project Detail
Infant immune development
Study design: PBMCs are collected longitudinally from mother–infant pairs at birth and at 2 weeks, 2, 6, and 12 months of age, and profiled by single-cell RNA-seq (mRNA expression) and single-nucleus ATAC-seq (chromatin accessibility) to track how immune cell states change over the first year of life.

What we're studying

SOX4⁺ naïve T cells: a population of naïve T cells highly enriched in infants that carries stemness and thymic-emigrant signatures, with a built-in bias toward Th2 differentiation.

Constitutive interferon signaling: infant T cells express interferon-stimulated genes (ISGs) at baseline, without any active infection — a kind of pre-armed antiviral state that may help compensate for an adaptive immune system that is still coming online.

Why it matters

Understanding what's normal — and distinctly infant — about early-life immunity is a prerequisite for understanding why infants respond differently to infections like RSV and SARS-CoV-2, and for designing vaccines and immune therapies that work with, rather than against, the unique biology of this life stage.

This work builds directly on our recent study, "Single-cell map of the human immune system across the lifespan reveals infant-specific immune signatures" (Nature Communications, 2026), and continues with follow-on projects in the lab.

Research Focus · Project Detail
SOX4⁺ T cell biology

What are SOX4⁺ T cells?

SOX4⁺ naïve T cells are a distinct population of naïve T cells markedly enriched in infants. They express a transcriptional program — including SOX4, ID3, and IKZF2 — associated with stemness, recent thymic emigrant status, and hypo-responsiveness, and they carry a built-in bias toward Th2 differentiation.

SOX4⁺ T cells carry a stemness and hypo-responsiveness program (recent thymic emigrants, tissue-resident in infant lungs and intestines, dysfunctional CAR T cells), contribute to humoral immunity by supporting ectopic lymphoid structures, may shape antibody responses across early life, and represent a potential target for improving vaccine responses, reducing infection susceptibility, and enhancing cancer immunotherapy.

What we're studying

Stemness and hypo-responsiveness: SOX4⁺ T cells share features with recent thymic emigrants and tissue-resident T cells in infant lungs and intestines, and related dysfunctional states have been described in CAR T cells.

Role in humoral immunity: through CXCL13 signaling, SOX4⁺ T cells can support the formation of ectopic lymphoid structures that shape antibody responses.

Role in early-life immunity: we are asking how the abundance of SOX4⁺ T cells across the first year of life relates to the maturation of antibody responses to infection and vaccination.

Why it matters

Because SOX4⁺ T cells are enriched in infants and linked to hypo-responsiveness, understanding their biology may help explain why early-life immune and vaccine responses differ from those in adults — and could point to strategies for enhancing vaccine responses, reducing susceptibility to infection, and improving the effectiveness of cell-based cancer therapies.
